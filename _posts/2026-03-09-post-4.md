Right before the Eid-ul-Azha vacations, the university announced mid-term results for Calculus and Digital Logic Design, and it turned out to be good timing to end the academic stretch on. I scored a full 40 out of 40 in Calculus, which felt like genuine validation of the hours I'd put into preparation, and 37 out of 40 in DLD, which gave me real confidence that my grasp of digital logic concepts was actually solid rather than just good enough to get by.

Going into the vacation itself, I didn't fully switch off from academics — I spent part of it working on an academic project under Dr. Bilal Ahmad's supervision, continuing to explore seismology, the study of earthquakes and seismic waves. He walked us through how earthquakes occur due to tectonic plate movement, and how scientists actually measure intensity using the Richter and Moment Magnitude scales — details that turn a topic you've heard about your whole life into something you can reason about quantitatively. He also guided us through dataset design: what fields actually matter for this kind of data, things like date and time, coordinates, depth, magnitude, location, and population density, and pointed us toward real sources like the USGS database. From there, I worked independently to design my own dataset, picking features that would be useful for both exploratory analysis and potential machine learning applications down the line. It reinforced something I was starting to notice about Dr. Bilal Ahmad's mentorship style — he gives you the structure and the reasoning behind it, then expects you to take it the rest of the way yourself.

The vacation also gave me time to commit to something I'd been wanting to build for a while — a Digital Stopwatch project for DLD, built around a 555 timer IC, 7490 decade counters, 7447 BCD-to-seven-segment decoders, and seven-segment displays. I sourced all the components myself from a local electronics shop, which turned out to be its own small lesson in patience, and then assembled the whole circuit on a breadboard. Inevitably, things didn't work the first time — loose connections and timing issues forced me to troubleshoot methodically instead of guessing, and that kind of hands-on debugging taught me things no textbook explanation really gets across.

Between strong mid-term results, a vacation that somehow turned into more learning rather than less, and a stopwatch that eventually ticked correctly after a fair bit of frustration, this stretch ended up being one of the more productive "breaks" I've had — proof that a vacation doesn't have to mean a full stop on momentum.
