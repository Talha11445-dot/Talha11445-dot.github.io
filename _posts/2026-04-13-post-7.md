This Eid-ul-Azha brought one of the more meaningful family traditions of the year. My father and brothers purchased two animals for Qurbani, fulfilling one of Islam's most significant rituals in remembrance of Prophet Ibrahim's (AS) sacrifice. After careful selection at the animal market in the days leading up to Eid, the sacrifice was performed following the Eid prayers, and the meat was divided into three portions — one for our own family, one for relatives and friends, and one for the poor. It's a tradition that does more than just mark the occasion; going through the whole process together, from selection to distribution, genuinely strengthens the sense of family bond and shared faith that the day is built around.

On the first day of Eid, after the morning prayers and the Qurbani itself, our family turned to preparing Dam Pukht — a slow-cooking method dating back to the Mughal era, with a name that literally means "cooked on steam." The mutton was marinated with yogurt, spices, and herbs, sealed into a pot, and left to cook slowly over hours rather than rushed on high heat. Opening that pot released an aroma that's hard to describe unless you've actually been in the room for it, and sharing the dish with family afterward turned into one of those moments that carries both spiritual significance and a simple kind of warmth that's hard to manufacture any other way.

Once the holiday wound down and exam preparation picked back up, I got my first genuine hands-on exposure to machine learning, working with my group on a project using the USA Car Dataset for Programming Fundamentals. We spent a good amount of time just cleaning and structuring the data before we ever got to training anything, and that turned out to be the real lesson — data preparation matters just as much as the algorithm sitting on top of it, maybe more. Training the model to make predictions afterward felt almost anticlimactic by comparison, precisely because most of the actual thinking had already happened during the cleaning and structuring stage. It connected directly to something Dr. Bilal Ahmad had emphasized repeatedly in Database Systems — that the value of any model or query is only as good as the quality and precision of the dataset feeding into it, which is part of why he consistently pushed us toward high-quality, real-world datasets in the first place.

Between a holiday spent with family and a project that quietly reshaped how I think about data, this stretch ended up tying together two very different parts of life that don't usually overlap on a blog like this — but both, in their own way, were about patience paying off slowly rather than all at once.
