Before the vacations ended, Dr. Bilal Ahmad gave every student in Database Systems a directive that, at the time, sounded like a formality but turned out to be anything but — complete all 14 DLD labs, properly documented, and have them spiral-bound by the end of the holidays. He was clear that this wasn't busywork; each lab built on lecture concepts in sequence, starting from basic gates and working all the way up to flip-flops and the open-ended Machine Safety Controller, and skipping the documentation step would mean losing the thread connecting all of it. Following his instructions, I made sure every single lab included an objective, an equipment list, circuit diagrams, truth tables, observations, and a conclusion, and then had the whole set spiral-bound at a local shop. Holding that bound stack afterward felt like holding an entire semester's practical learning in one place — which, in a literal sense, it was.

It was actually in Database Systems that I had a bit of a turning point this semester. Going in, I assumed the subject would be heavily theoretical — definitions, normal forms, abstract diagrams. That assumption broke the moment Dr. Bilal Ahmad introduced us to SQL. Writing a `SELECT` statement and watching exactly the data I asked for appear on screen made the whole subject feel tangible in a way theory alone never had. I started experimenting beyond what was actually assigned, just to see what else I could pull out of a dataset, and that shift in engagement ended up reshaping how I think about the entire field of computer engineering — database systems stopped looking like one course among many and started looking like a foundational skill sitting underneath almost all real-world software.

Meanwhile, our OOP instructor announced a comprehensive quiz covering everything from before and after the midterm, including GUI programming in C#. The pre-midterm material meant going back over classes, objects, constructors, and the four OOP pillars, while the post-midterm portion covered multilevel inheritance, method overriding and overloading, interfaces, and exception handling. The quiz was MCQ-based, which sounds easier on paper but actually demands more precise, unambiguous understanding than a written answer would — there's no partial credit for "roughly" knowing a concept. That pushed pretty much everyone, myself included, toward serious, focused revision rather than the usual last-minute skim.

Between finally seeing a semester's worth of DLD labs bound into one physical record, genuinely falling for SQL in a way I hadn't expected, and bracing for a quiz that demanded precision rather than approximation, this week was less about learning something brand new and more about consolidating everything that had already been building underneath the surface.
