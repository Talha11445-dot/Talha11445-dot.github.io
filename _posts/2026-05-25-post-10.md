With final exams set to begin on 15th June, I made a deliberate decision to start preparing on 29th May rather than drifting toward the usual last-minute scramble. That gave me roughly two and a half weeks to work through Digital Logic Design, Object Oriented Programming, Communication Skills, and the rest of the semester's subjects in a structured, balanced way. It sounds like a small decision, but starting that early changed the entire texture of exam prep — instead of panicking through a compressed week, I had room to actually plan which days went to which subject.

To make the most of that window, I adopted the 1-4-7 spaced repetition method — reviewing material on Day 1, coming back to it on Day 4, and then again on Day 7. It lines up with how the natural forgetting curve works, so each pass through the material took noticeably less time than the one before it, while still reinforcing long-term retention rather than short-term cramming. By the third exposure to a topic, it genuinely felt familiar rather than half-remembered, which translated into a kind of confident recall during the actual exams that I hadn't really experienced in earlier semesters.

That preparation paid off most clearly in Database Systems, where I scored 29 out of 40 on the final — the highest score in Section B. The paper had three major components: a genuinely difficult first question that seemed to challenge the entire class, a Normalization question I found manageable, and a Database Anomalies question I answered with real confidence. More than the number itself, the result felt like a marker of how far the subject had taken me over the semester — from initial skepticism about whether Database Systems would be useful at all, to a level of mastery shaped significantly by [Dr. Bilal Ahmad's](https://www.facebook.com/Dr.BilalAhm) structured teaching approach and his consistent emphasis on practical, real-world data applications rather than abstract theory for its own sake.

Starting early and studying deliberately isn't a particularly exciting story to tell, but the Database Systems result made it obvious that the boring, disciplined version of exam prep was the one that actually worked — proof that the method mattered just as much as the material itself.
