The DLD Project Exhibition on 8th June gave my Digital Stopwatch project — the one I'd built earlier over Eid vacations using a 555 timer, decade counters, and seven-segment displays — its actual moment in front of an audience. I spent the days before the exhibition testing the circuit thoroughly, partly to catch any remaining issues and partly to make sure I could explain its working mechanism confidently if a judge pushed on the details. When the Q&A session came, having that depth of familiarity paid off — it felt less like reciting a memorized explanation and more like genuinely walking someone through a system I understood end to end, which is probably the closest thing to a professional engineering presentation I've experienced so far.

Around the same time, a Communication Skills assignment asked us to put together a slide presentation on a technical topic of our choosing, and I picked the Karnaugh Map, partly because it tied in directly with what I was already deep into for DLD. I built the slides around K-Map construction, how to fill cells based on a truth table, and how to derive a minimized Boolean expression once the grouping is done. I leaned on diagrams and color-coded examples throughout, since K-Maps are inherently visual — trying to explain cell adjacency and grouping purely in words tends to confuse people who'd understand it instantly from a picture.

For our Database Systems final project, my group turned to a Kaggle dataset focused on the study of earthquakes, which felt like a natural continuation of the seismology dataset work I'd done earlier in the semester under Dr. Bilal Ahmad's guidance. This time, instead of just designing the schema, we structured the actual data into a proper relational database, applying normalization and defining key relationships between tables, and then wrote SQL queries to analyze patterns across magnitude, location, and depth. Watching raw rows of earthquake data turn into something genuinely interpretable — clusters by region, depth correlating with damage severity — reinforced something Dr. Bilal Ahmad had been emphasizing all semester: the value of working with real-world, precisely collected datasets instead of toy examples that don't reward careful analysis.

Three very different formats this week — a live demonstration, a slide deck, and a SQL-driven analysis — but all three were really about the same thing: taking something I understood privately and finding the right way to make it legible to someone else, whether that someone was a judge, a classroom, or just a query result on a screen.
