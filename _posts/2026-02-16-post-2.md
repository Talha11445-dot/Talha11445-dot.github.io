This week's Object Oriented Programming lab gave us something with real-world weight behind it — designing a University Admission Management System, the kind of software actually used by institutions to streamline their admission processes. Our instructor walked through the full scope before we started: an Applicant Registration Module, an Eligibility Checking Module, a Merit List Generation Module, an Admin Panel, and a Fee Voucher Generation Module. We built it around classes like `Applicant`, `Program`, `Department`, and `Admin`, each holding its own private data, with a base `Person` class and derived classes `Applicant` and `AdminUser` handling inheritance. The more interesting part was polymorphism — different program types needed to handle eligibility checks and merit calculation differently, but through the same shared interface. Most groups, mine included, used C++ for this one, and it pushed us to think analytically about design before writing a single line of implementation code.

Database Systems brought a very different kind of pressure this week. Dr. Bilal Ahmad gave us a directive that felt unusually real-world: finalize our semester projects within a single 3-hour lab session. He explained that this mirrors how database developers often work under strict deadlines professionally, and the constraint was deliberate rather than arbitrary. My project had a topic I genuinely found interesting — seismology, the study of earthquakes — and the goal was to design a fully functional relational database storing earthquake data: magnitude, location, depth, date, and damage intensity, spread across interconnected tables for Earthquake Records, Seismic Zones, and Damage Assessment, linked through primary and foreign keys. With that 3-hour window in mind, I focused everything on finalizing table structures, inserting sample records, testing queries, and getting documentation done — and came away with a much sharper sense of what time-boxed, focused execution actually feels like, which is a lesson that goes well beyond this one lab.

Digital Logic Design took us into sequential circuits this week, specifically software simulations of Latches, De-Latches, and Transparent Latches. A latch is a bistable multivibrator — it can hold one bit of binary information, which sounds simple until you actually trace how it works. The SR Latch, built from NAND or NOR gates, has Set and Reset inputs along with Q and Q' outputs. De-Latching is essentially resetting the latch out of its stored state. The part I found genuinely interesting was the Transparent Latch, or D Latch: when Enable is HIGH, the output Q follows the input D in real time, but the moment Enable goes LOW, the latch freezes and holds whatever value it last had. Simulating this made the idea of "memory" in digital circuits feel concrete rather than abstract — you could watch the exact moment a circuit stopped reacting to its input and started holding onto a value instead.

Three very different lessons came out of this week — designing a multi-module system from scratch in OOP, working under a hard deadline while building something I actually cared about in Database Systems, and watching a circuit "remember" a value for the first time in DLD. Different subjects, but all of them were really about the same underlying skill: structuring something so it behaves predictably under constraints, whether those constraints are a merit-calculation rule, a 3-hour clock, or an Enable signal going low.
