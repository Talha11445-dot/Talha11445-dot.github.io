During the Eid vacations, I used some of the free time to build something I'd genuinely enjoy working on — a Car Racing Game in C#, built in Visual Studio Community using Windows Forms. I designed separate classes for the player's car, the opponent cars, and the scoreboard, which gave me a real reason to apply encapsulation, inheritance, and abstraction outside of a structured lab assignment. I implemented keyboard controls, randomly generated opponents, a scoring system, and collision detection, and getting all of that working together — not just each piece in isolation — gave me a noticeably different kind of confidence than a guided lab project does. There's a difference between completing an assignment correctly and building something interactive that actually works the way you imagined it.

Programming Fundamentals, on the other hand, taught me a much less comfortable lesson this semester. When the course began, I treated the quizzes too lightly, assuming that my prior knowledge of C would carry over easily into Python. It didn't, at least not the way I expected. I did prepare for the mid exam, but my code didn't meet the standard our teacher, Dr. Bilal Ahmad, expected, and I ended up scoring 18.5 out of 30. It stung at the time, but it taught me something I probably needed to learn the hard way — prior knowledge from one language doesn't automatically transfer into mastery of another, and clean coding habits matter just as much as getting the right output. After that, I started practicing more deliberately and paying much closer attention to the coding standards he expected rather than just whether my code technically ran.

Back in DLD, Lab 14 brought one of the more substantial open-ended assignments of the semester — designing and implementing a Machine Safety Controller, a digital logic circuit meant to monitor real conditions like temperature, pressure, safety guards, and emergency stops. I used Boolean algebra and Karnaugh Maps to simplify the logic expressions before implementation, then built the circuit using AND, OR, NOT, and NAND gates, with flip-flops handling the shutdown-state latching. Successfully getting this to work felt like genuine proof that I could take digital logic principles and apply them, independently, to something resembling a real engineering problem rather than a textbook exercise.

This stretch ended up being a mix of confidence and correction — confidence from finishing the racing game and the safety controller on my own terms, and correction from a Programming Fundamentals score that forced me to take the course, and the standards behind it, more seriously than I had been.
