Going into my Database Systems mid-term, I'd spent the bulk of my preparation time on the theoretical chapters — definitions, concepts, the broader principles behind how database systems function. I felt confident walking in, assuming that solid theoretical grounding would carry me comfortably through the paper. What I hadn't anticipated was just how much weight the exam placed on Entity-Relationship Diagrams, a practical, visual modeling skill I'd reviewed only briefly compared to the depth I'd put into theory. When I saw the proportion of marks tied to ER Diagram questions, it became clear my preparation strategy had been somewhat lopsided. I had to slow down and work from first principles — identifying entities, attributes, and relationships on the spot — rather than leaning on memorized definitions that didn't directly apply. I managed to attempt the questions reasonably well, but the experience made it obvious that practical, diagram-based topics in Database Systems demand just as much deliberate practice as theory, not less, and it directly reinforced something [Dr. Bilal Ahmad](https://www.linkedin.com/in/drbilalphd/) had emphasized throughout the course — that database systems are ultimately about modeling and representing real data relationships accurately, not just memorizing concepts around them.

Not long after, our Database Systems teacher ran a separate quiz focused specifically on ER Diagrams, and given how exposed I'd just felt during the mid-term, I went into it with a noticeably different level of seriousness. The quiz asked us to translate real-world scenarios into proper ER models — identifying entities, defining relationships, and correctly representing cardinality and constraints. It was a genuinely different mental exercise from writing SQL queries or recalling definitions; it required visual, structural thinking about how data connects across a system, closer to sketching a blueprint than writing code. It reinforced, again, that balanced preparation matters in this subject, and that visual modeling skills shouldn't be treated as secondary to theory — a lesson that resurfaced later when I was designing the structure for both the seismology dataset and the final earthquake project.

After a semester defined by early quizzes, a humbling mid-term, a hands-on machine learning project, and a final exam that tested coding skill and conceptual understanding in roughly equal measure, I checked the LMS and saw a B+ in Programming Fundamentals. Looking back at everything that led to it, the grade felt earned rather than lucky. I thought back to the early overconfidence that cost me marks on the mid-term, the lesson in humility that followed, the long hours spent training a model on the USA Car Dataset for my first real machine learning project, and a final exam where a missed twenty-mark question on machine learning code reminded me, once again, that thorough revision has to cover every topic, not just the ones that feel comfortable. Getting a B+ after all of that felt deeply satisfying precisely because it wasn't built on prior familiarity with C — it came from genuine adaptation, consistent practice, and gradually better coding discipline under the standards Dr. Bilal Ahmad held us to throughout the course. It left me more confident as a Computer Engineering student and reinforced something worth holding onto going forward: a rough start, like that early mid-term score, doesn't determine the final outcome if it's met with honest reflection and real effort afterward.

Between an exam that exposed a gap in my preparation and a grade that proved a rough start doesn't have to define the ending, this stretch of the semester ended up being less about any single subject and more about how preparation, once it's actually balanced, eventually catches up to where it needs to be.
